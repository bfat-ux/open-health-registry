About OHR

What we are and why we exist.

A nonprofit infrastructure organization building the foundational data layer that national health systems in low-resource settings need — but rarely have the capacity to build on their own.

The problem

Digital health fails at a predictable point in many low- and middle-income countries: systems cannot reliably verify the people and places they are meant to connect. Is this pharmacist licensed? Does this clinic still exist? Is this the same patient who visited last month?

Without trustworthy answers, e-prescriptions get routed to the wrong person, referrals land at closed facilities, and patient records accumulate duplicates that clinicians cannot trust. The root cause is not a shortage of health software. It is a shortage of the foundational data layer — provider verification, facility registries, and patient identity resolution — that every digital health system depends on.

Many countries are adopting the OpenHIE architecture as their blueprint. While this provides a strong framework, the critical registry components are often built piecemeal, remain donor-dependent for years, or never achieve full national ownership.

What OHR builds

Open Health Registry builds and transfers that missing foundational layer:

  • Provider verification registries with structured trust levels
  • Facility databases with field verification and geospatial data
  • Patient identity resolution infrastructure that works across organizations without forcing centralization

Every component uses open standards — FHIR R4, OpenAPI 3.1, PostgreSQL + PostGIS — so there is never proprietary lock-in. The operating model is Build–Operate–Transfer: we build the system, run it through the transition period with local teams, and hand over complete ownership of code, data, and operational knowledge to the national authority. The country's data stays sovereign. The methodology stays with OHR and is reused in the next country.

How OHR is different

Most health informatics projects are designed to be maintained indefinitely by vendors or implementing partners. OHR is explicitly designed to become unnecessary.

We document for handover from day one. We train counterpart institutions during the operate phase. We define success as the moment the national authority no longer needs us. This "registry + transfer" focus sets us apart in a landscape where many initiatives deliver valuable components but leave countries with ongoing dependencies.

We also do not overstate progress. As of April 2026, OHR has one active program (the Nigeria Pharmacy Registry with 6,891 records), one regulatory conversation initiated with the Pharmacy Council of Nigeria (April 27, 2026), and no external users yet. We are building carefully and transparently.

Bernard Fatoye, MBBS, MPA

Founder & Executive Director, Open Health Registry

Nigerian-trained physician (MBBS, University of Ilorin) with a Master of Public Administration in Nonprofit Management from Texas A&M University. Bernard currently works as a Senior Clinical Informatics Specialist in the United States, where he has spent the last decade at the intersection of frontline medicine, health policy, and information systems.

He founded Open Health Registry after repeatedly seeing the same pattern: ambitious digital health projects in Nigeria and across Africa that failed not because of technology, but because the foundational data layer — verified providers, accurate facilities, and reliable patient identity — simply did not exist. OHR exists to close that gap with infrastructure that national systems can truly own.
